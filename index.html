About me.

Hi! I am Youjia. Broadly, I am interested in how people's interactions with media shape their imagination, aspirations, and decisions about their life trajectories. My current work sits at the intersection of sociology of culture, science and technology studies (with a focus on digital media), and sociology of work and occupations. I study how individuals construct career and educational aspirations through online interactions with strangers, particularly under conditions of labor-market uncertainty.

My research develops along two interconnected lines — linking micro-level interaction to macro-level social change. Theoretically, I build on cultural sociology's debates around motivation and justification, while drawing on pragmatism and phenomenology to examine how people imagine their futures in digitally mediated environments. Methodologically, I am interested in integrating semantic network analysis with large-scale text data to trace how cultural understandings of "promising" careers evolve over time, and how these perceptions are linked to cultural values.

Previously, my master's thesis examines how STEM-oriented vocational aspirations in China emerge through the interplay of institutional structures and online discourse. My undergraduate thesis focused on how individuals navigate career choices through online forums using qualitative methods. I am also the first author of a published article Nourishing social solidarity in exchanging gifts: a study on social exchange in Shanghai communities during COVID-19 lockdown in Humanities & Social Sciences Communications.
